Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Cheng-I Lin — chl236@ucsd.edu   Dillon Bowes — dmbowes@ucsd.edu   Vishal Paidi — vpaidi@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Developed Countries Outsource Carbon Emissions from Developing Countries in an Attempt to Portray Themseleves as Environmentally Friendly Nations.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Textual Annotations
    • Score: 1.5 (Earnest)
    • By including multiple annotations within the plot itself, we can highlight important years (like 2006) and provide concrete numbers, making the takeaway clearer and more accessible. This help guides the viewer’s attention to the crucial factors that support the main claim — outsourcing of emissions — by emphasizing and contextualizing the significant difference of net carbon imports during 2006 between developed and developing countries. We also considered just implementing the annotations as concise paragraphs under the graph title. However, this misses the opportunity to directly interpret the context of the specified years on the chart, limiting its effectiveness in allowing the viewers to grasp in which part of the graph is the annotations talking about (also increases the length of the above paragraph, making it too wordy). The only potential bias lies in emphasizing a particular year, which might overstate the importance of a single year, but the intent is explanatory rather than manipulative.
  • Color Contrast
    • Score: 1.5 (Earnest)
    • The use of blue and red colors makes a clear visual distinction between developed and developing countries, allowing viewers to better understand which line represents which classification. The color blue symbolizes stability and professionalism, fitting for developed countries. The color red symbolizes urgency and imbalance, reflecting the challenges encountered by developing countries. This color contrast also stresses the diverging differences between the two groups, helping viewers associate import as developed countries and export as developing countries. At first, we thought about using the color green for developed countries, but green might be affiliated with environmentally friendly solutions, which might stray the focus away from our main argument that argues against the eco-friendliness of developed countries. However, one possible downside of using the color red is that it may subconsciously suggest “bad,” slightly moralizing the developing countries’ role, but this was outweighed by clarity and interpretability.
  • Single Y-Axis
    • Score: 2 (Earnest)
    • Through a common y-axis for the net imports of both developed and developing countries, the viewers can get a better understanding of import and export carbon flows over the years with direct comparisons, making it obvious that developed nations import carbon (positive, above 0) and developing nations export carbon (negative, below 0). This also ensures that both trends are fairly evaluated with the same scale, preventing axis manipulations that exaggerate the results. Although we considered implementing two separate y-axes, we thought it would be less intuitive and less visually striking since both imports and exports follow similar trends. Therefore, we decided not to over complicate the visualization and used a single axis instead. Even though the single axis can provide clarity, it may also be confusing for viewers as to how a negative carbon net import corresponds to export when the phrase is literally import, not export. However, this is covered through the explanation on top of the graph, stating that Net Imports = Imports - Exports.
  • Horizontal Averages
    • Score: 2 (Earnest)
    • By displaying the mean values of net imports and exports for developed and developing countries, viewers can comprehend not just the trend of the annual net imports, but the summary of the long term trends as well. The averages help reinforce the fact that the observed pattern isn’t temporary or random, but rather a consistent trend of over 20 plus years. This is an honest technique aggregate complex data into a clear message that suggests that developed countries have always been importing carbon to lower their own carbon footprints, better persuading the viewers for our main claim. We also considered the use of trend lines to show the general direction of net imports over time, but the trends (positive/negative slopes) can be easily distinguished by viewers even without such lines. Moreover, the averages provide an additional data to better contextualize the volume of net imports between developed and developing countries that viewers can easily compare with. However, a possible downside is that averages can oversimplify temporal variations. Yet, their small text annotations and dotted lines prevent them from obscuring any relevant trends over the years.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Ratio Aggregation
    • Score: -2 (Deceptive)
    • By switching from net imports to shares of carbon production, we effectively changed the perspective of the data. This means that the attention of the viewers is now shifted away from imports and exports (carbon outsourcing), and instead highlights the carbon production ratio of developed and developing countries. What this does is that it provides a new way of calculating the data to emphasize proportionality rather than volume, downplaying the disparities in absolute emissions. This makes inequalities seem smaller than they actually are, persuading the viewers that even with the carbon outsourcing of developed countries, they are still generally on equal terms with developing countries when it comes to carbon emissions. We have also tried many different aggregations of the data, such as using rolling averages and changing country classifications, but this is by far the most effective portrayal of data that depicts the sameness in carbon production levels. However, a potential downside is that viewers may find ratios of over 1 confusing and difficult to interpret. This unusal high value is due to the equation of subtracting exported carbon when calculating domestic production. With high exports, the denominator becomes smaller and thus the calculated value becomes larger (over 1), which is counter intuitive to how a ratio or percentage works.
  • Y-Axis Manipulation
    • Score: -1.5 (Deceptive)
    • Using an extended y-axis, all the values from the graph are compressed together, creating a visualization that both trends are similar as they are closer to one another. This better persuades the argument that the differences between developed and developing countries are minor (thus carbon outsourcing do not create major impacts) as the viewers can easily tell that the two trends are plotted in similar regions of the y-axis. Besides the extension of the y-axis, we also thought about the compression of the y-axis to make the differences seem larger than usual. By doing so, we can argue that developed countries have less carbon production than developing countries so that they are indeed eco-friendly. However, we thought that it was ultimately better to indicate sameness as greater production share would mean greater export (value > 1: exports dominate net trade so domestic production exceeds net footprint) that help argue for, not against, the proposition of carbon outsourcing. Although this axis manipulation helps persuade viewers upon first glance, experienced viewers may catch on this deception quickly, and may thus lose credibility in our argument.
  • Subtitle Information
    • Score: -1.5 (Deceptive)
    • Through the utilization of subtitles, we only included key information that would help the viewers see the graph through the lens that imports and exports (and thus carbon outsourcing) are nonexistent. By only showing what the ratio value of close to 1 indicates, viewers are effectively drawn to the fact that all emissions came from domestic production, not from imports or exports. This helps strengthen the idea that the emission levels of developed and developing countries are similar while removing the idea that developed countries outsources carbon from developing countries. Basically, it hides the meaning behind the rato values that are below (import) or above (export) 1 so that the viewers are only focused on the simialrity of eco-friendly carbon production, and not on carbon outsourcing. However, experienced viewers may still notice this trend of ratio values, thus losing credibility for our plot. Although we tried to use a horizontal dashed line to indicate the ratio value of 1 and what it represents, we found that it might cause overplotting issues as we already squished both lines into a manipulated y-axis. Therefore, adding an extra line in the middle of the tiny space between the two compressed trends would make it extremely difficult to see and comprehend.

Final Reflection

Overall, our design process was pretty straightforward: it is to show the difference in net imported carobon between developed and developing countries over the years. Therefore, we settled on using a line graph to better show the trends of how nations import or export carbon emissions. By doing so, we can effectively visualize how developed countries are importing carbon from developing countries to decrease their own national carbon emission levels, thus making them look more eco-friendly. However, the more difficult part was to make a deceptive visualization that argues the opposite. We had many ideas as to how to aggregate and portray the data to show that both developed and developing countries have similar levels of emissions despite the imports and exports. For instance, we thought of using rolling averages to minimize peaks, changing country classification to include china and india as their gross outputs are considered developed even though that isn't the case per capita, and altering y-axis values to percent changes. At the end, we settled for using domestic emission production shares. This is because, surprisingly, both developed and developing countries have similar domestic carbon emission shares, meaning that changing the aggregation calculation technique to carbon production shares generates the insight that developed countries are indeed eco-friendly.

We think that the definition of “ethical analysis and visualization” is the development of visualizations through fair, thoughtful, and truthful representations. This means we not only have to ensure accuracy in our visualizations, but also be mindful for how design choices can shape interpretation. Therefore, an ethical visualization strives to represent data faithfully, respect audiences, acknowledge uncertainty and limitations, and consider the impacts of interpretations, meaning that it is a balance of technical accuracy and moral responsibility. Although it is difficult to draw any bounds on what is considered to be “fair” and “truthful” when one is trying to effectively persuade the other, I think that one bound is data integrity. This means that we should always attempt to achieve clarity while preserving proportional relationships. Therefore, it is “acceptable” to remove a few extreme outliers to simplify data, to not let certain flukes or random chances affect the overall visualization. However, it becomes misleading when you manipulate axis, omit large sections of data, or cherry-pick certain data to purposefully create non-existing trends. That being said, sometimes persuasion and ethics can’t be cleanly separated. Thus, it is always best to reflect upon your design choices before publishing your work.
