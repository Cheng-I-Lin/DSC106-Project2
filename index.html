Proposition: Developed Countries Outsource Carbon Emissions from Developing Countries in an Attempt to Portray Themseleves as Environmentally Friendly Nations.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Textual Annotations
    • Score: 1.5 (Earnest)
    • By including multiple annotations within the plot itself, we can highlight important years (like 2006) and provide concrete numbers, making the takeaway clearer and more accessible. This help guides the viewer’s attention to the crucial factors that support the main claim — outsourcing of emissions — by emphasizing and contextualizing the significant difference of net carbon imports during 2006 between developed and developing countries. We also considered just implementing the annotations as concise paragraphs under the graph title. However, this misses the opportunity to directly interpret the context of the specified years on the chart, limiting its effectiveness in allowing the viewers to grasp in which part of the graph is the annotations talking about (also increases the length of the above paragraph, making it too wordy). The only potential bias lies in emphasizing a particular year, which might overstate the importance of a single year, but the intent is explanatory rather than manipulative.
  • Color Contrast
    • Score: 1.5 (Earnest)
    • The use of blue and red colors makes a clear visual distinction between developed and developing countries, allowing viewers to better understand which line represents which classification. The color blue symbolizes stability and professionalism, fitting for developed countries. The color red symbolizes urgency and imbalance, reflecting the challenges encountered by developing countries. This color contrast also stresses the diverging differences between the two groups, helping viewers associate import as developed countries and export as developing countries. At first, we thought about using the color green for developed countries, but green might be affiliated with environmentally friendly solutions, which might stray the focus away from our main argument that argues against the eco-friendliness of developed countries. However, one possible downside of using the color red is that it may subconsciously suggest “bad,” slightly moralizing the developing countries’ role, but this was outweighed by clarity and interpretability.
  • Single Y-Axis
    • Score: 2 (Earnest)
    • Through a common y-axis for the net imports of both developed and developing countries, the viewers can get a better understanding of import and export carbon flows over the years with direct comparisons, making it obvious that developed nations import carbon (positive, above 0) and developing nations export carbon (negative, below 0). This also ensures that both trends are fairly evaluated with the same scale, preventing axis manipulations that exaggerate the results. Although we considered implementing two separate y-axes, we thought it would be less intuitive and less visually striking since both imports and exports follow similar trends. Therefore, we decided not to over complicate the visualization and used a single axis instead. Even though the single axis can provide clarity, it may also be confusing for viewers as to how a negative carbon net import corresponds to export when the phrase is literally import, not export. However, this is covered through the explanation on top of the graph, stating that Net Imports = Imports - Exports.
  • Horizontal Averages
    • Score: 2 (Earnest)
    • By displaying the mean values of net imports and exports for developed and developing countries, viewers can comprehend not just the trend of the annual net imports, but the summary of the long term trends as well. The averages help reinforce the fact that the observed pattern isn’t temporary or random, but rather a consistent trend of over 20 plus years. This is an honest technique aggregate complex data into a clear message that suggests that developed countries have always been importing carbon to lower their own carbon footprints, better persuading the viewers for our main claim. We also considered the use of trend lines to show the general direction of net imports over time, but the trends (positive/negative slopes) can be easily distinguished by viewers even without such lines. Moreover, the averages provide an additional data to better contextualize the volume of net imports between developed and developing countries that viewers can easily compare with. However, a possible downside is that averages can oversimplify temporal variations. Yet, their small text annotations and dotted lines prevent them from obscuring any relevant trends over the years.

Final Reflection

Overall, our design process was pretty straightforward: it is to show the difference in net imported carobon between developed and developing countries over the years. Therefore, we settled on using a line graph to better show the trends of how nations import or export carbon emissions. By doing so, we can effectively visualize how developed countries are importing carbon from developing countries to decrease their own national carbon emission levels, thus making them look more eco-friendly. However, the more difficult part was to make a deceptive visualization that argues the opposite. We had many ideas as to how to aggregate and portray the data to show that both developed and developing countries have similar levels of emissions despite the imports and exports. For instance, we thought of using rolling averages to minimize peaks, changing country classification to include china and india as their gross outputs are considered developed even though that isn't the case per capita, and altering y-axis values to percent changes.

We think that the definition of “ethical analysis and visualization” is the development of visualizations through fair, thoughtful, and truthful representations. Although it is difficult to draw any bounds on what is considered to be “fair” and “truthful” when one is trying to persuade the other without misleading them, I think that one bound is that visualizations should include almost all valid datapoints. This is because in the case of Graph 3, I purposefully removed a lot of data from 1970 to 2004 just to show a trend that occurred for a short period of time instead of showing the entire picture, which can mislead people into believing that data from 2005 to 2020 represents all historical data. However, I believe that it is “acceptable” to remove a few data points (just not entire sections of data), especially extreme outliers, to not let certain “flukes” or “random chances” affect the overall visualization.
